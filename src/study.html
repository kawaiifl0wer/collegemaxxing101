AP Capstone

🔬
AP Capstone

Capstone is a two-year program that develops research, writing, and presentation skills through independent inquiry.

2 courses · AP Seminar → AP Research

"The quality of your research question matters far more than your results."

🔬 AP Research
Medium ▼

AP Research is a year-long independent research course where you produce an academic paper and present it to a panel. The quality of your research question and methodology matters far more than your results.

  • Have a clear, narrow research gap by second semester — it's one of the most common points of failure.
  • Do not wait until the week before to start writing. Consistent writing from February onward is the only way to finish strong.
  • Avoid survey-based research. It requires extensive IRB paperwork and typically yields limited, non-generalizable results.
  • Social science approaches — such as data mining with a theoretical framework, or critical textual analysis — tend to be both manageable and impressive.
  • Use the five sample papers on the College Board website as direct models for structure and tone.
  • A null result or hypothesis that isn't supported is completely acceptable — graders care about process, not outcome.
College Board → r/APStudents →
🗣️ AP Seminar
Easy–Medium ▼

AP Seminar is a discussion and research-based course focused on analyzing multiple perspectives on complex topics. Performance depends heavily on your group work and individual writing.

  • Choose group members carefully — collaborative assignments make up a large portion of your grade.
  • Pick a topic that genuinely interests you; sustained engagement over the year is much easier when you care about the subject.
  • Do not procrastinate on the Team Multimedia Presentation or Individual Research Report — both require significant revision time.
College Board → Portfolio Guide →

Arts

🎨
Arts

Portfolio-based AP arts courses reward creative process, sustained inquiry, and thoughtful written evidence — not just finished artwork.

5 courses available

"You do not need to be an exceptional artist — you need to think artistically."

🎨AP 2-D Art and Design
Medium▼

This portfolio-based course has three components: a Sustained Investigation, Selected Works, and Written Evidence. You do not need to be an exceptional artist — you need to think artistically and explore a clear inquiry.

  • Upload all process work (thumbnails, sketches, studies, notes) alongside finished pieces — College Board wants to see your creative process, not just the end result.
  • Do not skimp on the written evidence. Explaining your intent, process, and artistic growth is just as important as the artwork itself.
  • Use art terminology (color, balance, form, line, etc.) in your written responses wherever possible.
  • For Selected Works, choose your five strongest pieces overall — they do not have to be from AP 2D and can include personal work.
  • Experiment with mediums early. Photography, collage, and mixed media are all valid.
College Board → Portfolio Examples →
🗿AP 3-D Art and Design
Medium▼

A portfolio-based course emphasizing three-dimensional work, from ceramics to sculpture to mixed-media installation. No prior ceramics experience is required, but expect a learning curve with new materials.

  • Start building your portfolio early and document everything — moodboards, sketches, and progress photos all count as process work.
  • Vary your materials and techniques across your Sustained Investigation to demonstrate versatility.
  • Written evidence is heavily weighted. Be concise and make sure every word connects to your inquiry.
College Board →
✏️AP Drawing
Medium▼

AP Drawing rewards students who demonstrate a clear, sustained artistic inquiry supported by diverse media and strong written reflections.

  • Use multiple traditional mediums — College Board does not favor portfolios consisting solely of digital or graphic art.
  • Progress shots and planning pages count toward your portfolio.
  • Your artwork must be guided by your inquiry — not simply related to it.
College Board →
🏺AP Art History
Medium-Hard▼

AP Art History requires memorization of 250 required works of art alongside strong analytical writing skills for FRQs.

  • Memorize the content, context, and function for all 250 required works. There is no shortcut here.
  • Use Quizlet — there are great community-made AP Art History decks covering every required work.
  • Practice FRQs extensively. The exam includes 6 FRQ types, and timed practice is the most effective preparation.
College Board → Khan Academy → Quizlet →
🎵AP Music Theory
Hard▼

AP Music Theory is uniquely challenging because of its aural component — you must hear, identify, and notate music under significant time pressure.

  • Speed up your MCQ pace in practice. There is very little time between audio clips on exam day.
  • Practice harmonic dictation regularly. Focus on training your ear to hear the bass line.
  • Print out released FRQs and practice with physical paper to better simulate exam conditions.
College Board → musictheory.net →

English

📰
English

AP English courses develop rhetorical analysis, argumentation, close reading, and timed essay writing — skills that transfer directly to college writing.

2 courses · Lang & Lit

"Analyze choices, not devices."

📰AP English Language and Composition
Medium▼

AP Lang focuses on rhetorical analysis, argumentation, and synthesis writing. The exam rewards students who analyze rhetorical choices in depth rather than listing devices superficially.

  • Always read the italicized introductory text before the passage — it hands you the rhetorical situation directly.
  • Analyze choices, not devices. Instead of "the author uses a metaphor," write "the author compares X to Y in order to..."
  • Select two major rhetorical choices and analyze them deeply rather than listing six devices superficially.
Past FRQs →
📖AP English Literature and Composition
Hard▼

AP Lit demands genuine close-reading ability, strong essay writing under time pressure, and familiarity with a wide range of literary works.

  • Take detailed, original notes throughout the year — avoid LitCharts and SparkNotes, which prevent analytical depth.
  • Read at least one Shakespeare play thoroughly. Shakespeare's density prepares you to handle any passage the exam presents.
  • Practice timed essay writing all year and cap practice sessions at 40 minutes per essay, no exceptions.
Past FRQs →

History & Social Sciences

🌐
History & Social Sciences

These courses blend content knowledge with analytical writing. DBQs, LEQs, and FRQs reward students who understand themes and patterns — not just isolated facts.

10 courses available

"Themes, not timelines."

✊AP African American Studies
Easy–Medium▼

A relatively new AP course covering the history, culture, and contributions of African Americans from the African diaspora to the present day.

  • Engage seriously with primary sources and theoretical frameworks — this course rewards critical analysis over surface-level recall.
  • Practice the FRQ format early using released materials on the College Board website.
  • Connect themes across time periods rather than treating each unit in isolation.
College Board →
🗺️AP Comparative Government and Politics
Easy–Medium▼

AP Comp Gov covers the political systems of six countries: China, Iran, Mexico, Nigeria, Russia, and the UK.

  • Create a comparison chart for each of the six countries across key dimensions.
  • The FRQ section often asks you to compare two or more countries directly — practice this format explicitly.
  • Keep up with current events in the six countries, as the exam regularly includes contemporary examples.
College Board →
🌐AP European History
Hard▼

AP Euro covers over 600 years of European history and requires both strong content knowledge and advanced essay writing skills, including the DBQ and LEQ.

  • Focus on thematic patterns (state building, religion, economics, social change) rather than memorizing isolated events.
  • Practice DBQ document annotation: identify HAPP for each source.
  • Write at least five timed practice LEQs and DBQs before exam day.
College Board → Heimler's History →
🧭AP Human Geography
Easy–Medium▼

AP Human Geography is commonly a student's first AP course. Vocabulary-heavy but conceptually approachable.

  • Master the vocabulary. A large share of MCQ questions can be answered correctly if you know key terms cold.
  • Practice applying concepts to real-world examples — FRQs ask you to use specific evidence, not just define terms.
  • Flashcards are particularly effective for this course given its vocabulary load.
College Board → Mr. Sinn →
📈AP Macroeconomics
Easy–Medium▼

AP Macro covers the economy as a whole — GDP, inflation, unemployment, fiscal policy, and monetary policy.

  • Master the core graphs: the PPC, AD/AS model, and Money Market — they appear constantly on both MCQs and FRQs.
  • Practice drawing and labeling graphs quickly and accurately.
  • Take as many released FRQs as possible — the format is highly repetitive.
College Board → Khan Academy →
📉AP Microeconomics
Easy▼

AP Micro examines individual consumers, firms, and markets. It has one of the higher rates of 5s among AP exams.

  • Learn and practice all the key supply and demand graphs, as well as cost curves for firms.
  • Understand market structures (perfect competition, monopoly, oligopoly) thoroughly, including MR = MC.
  • Complete all available past FRQs from College Board and check your responses against the scoring guidelines.
College Board → Khan Academy →
🧠AP Psychology
Easy▼

AP Psychology is widely considered one of the most approachable AP courses, covering major theories and research in psychology.

  • Vocabulary is the core of this exam. Build flashcard decks for each unit and review them consistently.
  • FRQs are straightforward — they ask you to define terms and apply them to a scenario.
  • Learn all major theorists and their associated theories. Confusing Freud with Erikson or Skinner with Bandura is a common and costly mistake.
College Board → Mr. Sinn →
🏛️AP United States Government and Politics
Medium▼

AP Gov covers the structure and function of U.S. government. The 15 required foundational documents and 9 required Supreme Court cases are tested heavily.

  • Know all 15 required documents and 9 required Supreme Court cases thoroughly.
  • The Argument Essay FRQ requires you to write a structured argument using a required document.
  • Distinguish between the formal powers and informal powers of each branch.
College Board → Khan Academy → Heimler's History →
🇺🇸AP United States History (APUSH)
Hard▼

APUSH covers American history from 1491 to the present across nine chronological periods. It demands both content knowledge and strong analytical writing.

  • Focus on understanding broad historical themes and patterns rather than memorizing isolated dates and facts.
  • Practice DBQ document annotation from early in the year: identify historical context, audience, purpose, and point of view.
  • Write and outline practice LEQs and SAQs regularly.
College Board → Khan Academy → Heimler's History →
🌏AP World History: Modern
Medium-Hard▼

AP World History covers global history from 1200 CE to the present. The scope is vast, and the DBQ and LEQ essay formats require strong analytical writing.

  • Learn the five course themes and use them to organize your knowledge.
  • Prioritize the DBQ. It requires you to incorporate documents, outside evidence, and contextualization all in one essay.
  • Start practicing FRQs earlier than you think you need to.
College Board → Heimler's History → Freeman Pedia →

Math

∫
Math

AP Math courses range from Precalculus through Calculus BC and Statistics. Conceptual understanding and timed practice are more valuable than formula memorization alone.

4 courses available

"Don't just memorize steps — understand why each technique works."

∫AP Calculus AB
Medium▼

AP Calculus AB covers limits, derivatives, and integrals — roughly equivalent to one semester of college calculus.

  • Make sure your precalculus and algebra foundations are solid before diving in.
  • Create and review flashcards for derivative rules, integral formulas, and key theorems throughout the year.
  • Practice FRQs weekly and review scoring guidelines carefully.
College Board → Khan Academy → Project Explained →
∑AP Calculus BC
Hard▼

AP Calculus BC covers all of AB plus parametric equations, polar functions, vector-valued functions, and infinite series.

  • Do not just memorize steps — understand why each technique works.
  • Series convergence and polar/parametric functions are BC-only topics that require extra time. Start on them early.
  • Practice timed MCQs regularly. The non-calculator section averages two minutes per question.
College Board → Khan Academy → Project Explained →
📐AP Precalculus
Easy–Medium▼

AP Precalculus covers polynomial, rational, exponential, logarithmic, and trigonometric functions with an emphasis on modeling.

  • Master function transformations — shifting, stretching, and reflecting graphs appears throughout the course.
  • This course serves as strong preparation for AP Calculus. Treat it seriously.
  • Practice constructing and interpreting mathematical models from real-world scenarios.
College Board → The Algebros → Michael Porinchak →
📊AP Statistics
Medium▼

AP Statistics covers data collection, probability, statistical inference, and interpretation. Writing clear explanations in context is as important as calculations.

  • Memorize FRQ sentence structures — statistical conclusions must be stated in context with appropriate language.
  • Always interpret your results in context. An answer with no interpretation often earns zero credit.
  • For inference procedures, develop a checklist routine: state the procedure, check conditions, calculate, and conclude.
College Board → Khan Academy → Michael Porinchak →

Computer Science

💻
Computer Science

AP CS courses range from broad computing concepts to full object-oriented programming in Java. Both reward consistent hands-on practice over passive reading.

2 courses · CSP & CS A

"Write and run code daily, even for small problems."

☕AP Computer Science A
Medium▼

AP CS A teaches object-oriented programming in Java, including data structures, algorithms, and program design.

  • Write and run code daily, even for small problems. Reading code without writing it is not sufficient preparation.
  • Master the Java Quick Reference sheet provided during the exam.
  • Trace through programs manually (on paper) to develop debugging intuition.
College Board → CodeHS →
💻AP Computer Science Principles
Easy▼

AP CSP is the most approachable AP course by student ratings. It covers broad computing concepts without requiring deep programming knowledge.

  • The Create Performance Task requires you to submit a working program with video and written responses. Start it well before the deadline.
  • Focus on understanding the conceptual vocabulary (abstraction, iteration, Boolean logic, binary, etc.).
  • The MCQ exam is generally straightforward for students who engage with the material throughout the year.
College Board → Code.org →

Sciences

🧬
Sciences

AP Science courses reward conceptual reasoning, lab-based thinking, and strong FRQ writing. Finishing content early to allow a full month of review is the most consistent predictor of success.

7 courses available

"Aim to finish content by March so you have a full month to review."

🧬 AP Biology
Hard ▼

AP Biology is one of the most content-heavy AP courses, spanning molecules to ecosystems.

  • Aim to finish the course content by March so you have 1–2 months dedicated entirely to review.
  • Practice past FRQs consistently — College Board reuses similar conceptual questions across years.
  • Use flashcards for vocabulary and active recall for concepts.
College Board → Bozeman Science → Khan Academy → AP Bio Penguins →
⚗️AP Chemistry
Hard▼

AP Chemistry is widely considered one of the most demanding AP courses, emphasizing conceptual understanding and problem-solving over pure memorization.

  • Do not take AP Chem without prior chemistry experience.
  • Focus on understanding why reactions happen, not just how to balance equations.
  • Practice interpreting graphs, data tables, and experimental results.
College Board → Khan Academy → Jeremy Krug →
♻️AP Environmental Science
Easy–Medium▼

AP Environmental Science is considered one of the more accessible AP science courses, but many students underestimate the quantitative component.

  • Do not ignore the math. Calculation-based FRQs appear every year.
  • Learn current environmental legislation (Clean Air Act, Clean Water Act, ESA, etc.).
  • Current events and real-world case studies are fair game.
College Board → Khan Academy → Mr. Smedes →
⚡AP Physics 1: Algebra-Based
Hard▼

AP Physics 1 has one of the lowest pass rates of any AP exam (~47%) because it demands genuine conceptual reasoning, not just formula application.

  • Focus on understanding physical relationships conceptually. Plugging numbers into formulas without understanding will not earn full credit on FRQs.
  • Practice explaining your reasoning in complete sentences.
  • Work through as many FRQs as possible.
College Board → Mr. M's World → Khan Academy →
🔋AP Physics 2: Algebra-Based
Hard▼

AP Physics 2 covers fluid dynamics, thermodynamics, electricity, magnetism, optics, and quantum/atomic physics.

  • AP Physics 1 is a strong prerequisite. Revisit those fundamentals if needed.
  • Electricity and magnetism is typically the most challenging unit — allocate additional study time here.
  • Practice diagram-based questions extensively: circuit, ray, and field line drawings all appear on FRQs.
College Board → Khan Academy → Mr. M's World →
🔌AP Physics C: Electricity and Magnetism
Very Hard▼

AP Physics C: E&M is calculus-based and considered the hardest AP science course, covering electric fields, Gauss's law, circuits, magnetic fields, and electromagnetic induction.

  • Concurrent enrollment in AP Calculus BC is essentially required.
  • Master Gauss's Law, Faraday's Law, and Ampere's Law.
  • Work through every available past FRQ.
College Board → Mr. M's World →
🚀AP Physics C: Mechanics
Very Hard▼

AP Physics C: Mechanics covers kinematics, Newton's laws, energy, momentum, rotation, and oscillation using calculus.

  • Calculus is used throughout — derivatives and integrals appear in kinematics, work, and rotational dynamics problems.
  • Rotational dynamics is the most difficult unit for most students. Begin practicing it well before the exam.
  • Draw clear, labeled free-body diagrams for every mechanics problem.
College Board → Mr. M's World →

🌍
World Languages

AP Language exams test all four skills: reading, writing, listening, and speaking. Daily immersion in authentic media is the single most effective way to prepare.

8 languages offered

"Consume authentic media daily — it builds fluency textbooks can't."

🇨🇳AP Chinese Language and Culture
Easy (for native/heritage speakers)▼

AP Chinese has the highest pass rate of any AP exam (~89%) largely because most test-takers are heritage or native speakers.

  • Practice the speaking components separately — formal presentations and interpersonal conversations require specific register even for fluent speakers.
  • Study cultural topics systematically: festivals, traditions, geography, and modern Chinese society.
  • Engage with Chinese media (news, shows, podcasts) regularly to maintain listening comprehension.
  • Side Note: If you're really desperate, you can download 小红书 and scroll there instead of TikTok or Instagram to practice everyday Chinese skills.

🇫🇷AP French Language and Culture
Medium▼

AP French tests all four language skills and cultural knowledge of the French-speaking world.

  • Consume French-language media daily — news broadcasts, films, and podcasts build authentic listening comprehension far better than textbook exercises.
  • Practice the Presentational Speaking task specifically.
  • Build vocabulary for cultural and societal themes (environment, technology, family, education).

🇩🇪AP German Language and Culture
Medium▼

AP German follows the same format as other AP language exams, testing all four language skills.

  • Engage with German-language media regularly.
  • Pay close attention to case endings and verb conjugations — grammatical accuracy affects scores on written and spoken tasks.
  • Practice writing structured argumentative essays in German.

🇮🇹AP Italian Language and Culture
Medium▼

AP Italian is one of the less commonly offered AP language exams, covering all four language skills and Italian cultural knowledge.

  • Immerse yourself in Italian media — films, RAI news, and Italian music are all effective.
  • Practice all four exam tasks in a balanced way.
  • Focus on cultural themes (art, history, family structure, food).

🇯🇵AP Japanese Language and Culture
Medium▼

AP Japanese tests all four language skills with an emphasis on formal communication. Mastery of hiragana, katakana, and common kanji is essential.

  • Be proficient in typing Japanese using both hiragana/katakana input and kanji conversion — the written exam tasks are computer-based.
  • Practice formal writing registers (teineigo and keigo) specifically.
  • Consume Japanese-language media (NHK Web Easy is excellent).

🏛️AP Latin
Hard▼

AP Latin focuses on translating and analyzing passages from Caesar's Gallic Wars and Vergil's Aeneid.

  • Read and translate the required texts repeatedly throughout the year, not just in the weeks before the exam.
  • Memorize the required vocabulary lists.
  • Practice sight-reading starting early in the year to build translation fluency beyond the required texts.

🇪🇸AP Spanish Language and Culture
Medium▼

AP Spanish Language is one of the most popular AP courses, testing all four language skills with authentic Spanish-language sources.

  • Listen to Spanish-language podcasts, news (BBC Mundo, NPR en Español), and shows.
  • Practice speaking regularly — record yourself on the presentational tasks and listen back critically.
  • Build vocabulary around recurring thematic areas: families, communities, science and technology, environment, and beauty/aesthetics.

📜AP Spanish Literature and Culture
Hard▼

AP Spanish Lit requires reading, analyzing, and writing about a required list of Spanish and Latin American literary works in Spanish.

  • Read and annotate all required texts carefully. Understanding historical and cultural context significantly improves analysis.
  • Practice the AP-style essay tasks in Spanish — literary commentary, comparison essays, and textual analysis — under timed conditions.
  • Learn literary terminology in Spanish (el narrador, el motivo, el tono, la ironía, etc.).

Study Methods

03

Working hard isn't enough — working smart is. These research-backed techniques will help you retain more, stress less, and actually feel prepared on exam day.

01
Active Recall

Instead of re-reading notes, test yourself on the material. Close the book and write down everything you remember. This forces your brain to retrieve information, which is how long-term memory forms. Flashcard apps like Anki are great for this.

02
Spaced Repetition

Study a topic, then revisit it after 1 day, then 3 days, then a week. This "spacing effect" dramatically boosts retention compared to cramming. Anki automates this for you based on how well you know each card.

03
The Pomodoro Technique

Study for 25 minutes, then take a 5-minute break. Repeat 4 times, then take a longer 15–30 minute break. This prevents burnout and keeps your focus sharp throughout a long study session.

04
The Feynman Technique

Pick a concept. Explain it out loud as if you're teaching it to a 5-year-old. Wherever you get stuck is exactly where your understanding has gaps — go back and fill them in. Then try again.

05
Mind Mapping

Draw a central idea in the middle of a blank page, then branch out with related concepts, subtopics, and connections. Especially useful for history, biology, and any subject with lots of interconnected ideas.

"The secret to studying effectively isn't working longer — it's working in a way that matches how your brain actually learns."
